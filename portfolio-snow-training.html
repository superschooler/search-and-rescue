Snow Training & Rescues

As a Mountain Team, we need to prepare for all terrains and weather conditions, and the snow is no exception. Admittedly, this is the hardest aspect for our team to train because Ventura County gets very little snow - an average of 0". However, Mount Pinos is in the northernmost segment of Ventura County and, at nearly 9,000 feet elevation, averages 1 to 2 meters of snow each year. As this is a part of Fillmore's search area, we must stay prepared for such conditions to be able to save lives when needed.

To become a Mountain Rescue Association accredited team, you must pass three separate tests - wilderness search, high-angle rescue, and snow & ice rescue. Fillmore Search & Rescue has already completed the first two accreditations and is looking to complete the third in the winter of 2019/2020 to receive our certification - arguably the Gold Standard of Search & Rescue teams. Snow Equipment

Some critical pieces of snow equipment we carry are snowshoes, crampons, and ice axes. We must regularly train in the use of all our gear, so we're ready whenever a call-out comes.

Info
Snowcat

Our Search and Rescue team has two snowcats for when the roads get closed or dangerous to drive on. These vehicles allow us to safely navigate snowy or icy roads or wide trails, saving us time and energy for our search.

Info
Jef Dye Memorial Box

After our team member, Jef Dye, was killed on February 3, 2019, we created a small remembrance kit to leave at Sawmill Mountain - the highest point in Kern County. We chose this location as it requires a bit further hike than Mount Pinos, the highest point in Ventura County, so it is a slightly less trafficked location and a higher peak, not to mention the view. If you happen to come across this, we ask that you be respectful of it and invite you to leave a note in the journal if you had a connection to Jef.

Mount Pinos

When it's a wet winter, there will be plenty of snow on Mount Pinos for us to practice snowshoeing and cross-country skiing. We'll use any and every method available to us to navigate snowy terrain and find lost or injured hikers quickly.

Info
Steep Slopes

Steep slopes like this are the perfect place to train in the use of crampons and ice axes. Just as people don't get lost when the weather is beautiful, people also don't get lost where it's easy to walk. Therefore, we periodically train in The Sierra, where the mountains are tall, and the slopes are harsh.
